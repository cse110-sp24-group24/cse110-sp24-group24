## Discussion Points & Notes

**1. Review of the First 50 Runs**
* Before this meeting, the team had already finished the initial 50 runs by splitting them up among group members.
* We started by comparing those results in person and looking at how much the outputs differed from one another.
* Some versions worked better in the browser than others, and there was a noticeable range in both code quality and how complete the apps felt.
* A big part of the discussion was making sure we judged the results based on the rules of the assignment, especially since we were not allowed to hand-edit anything.

**2. Choosing Candidates to Move Forward**
* After reviewing the baseline runs, we talked through which ones seemed strongest enough to move into the next round.
* We mainly looked at whether the app actually worked, how polished it felt, and whether the generated files were usable as-is.
* We also kept in mind that this was supposed to be an experiment, not just a search for the prettiest or most complete slot machine.
* Because of that, we paid attention to variation between runs and treated broken or weaker outputs as useful data instead of something to ignore.

**3. Completing the Refinement Rounds**
* During this meeting, we ended up finishing all of the remaining iteration rounds after the original 50 runs.
* At each stage, we discussed which candidates should keep moving forward and why.
* Those conversations usually came down to what each version did well, what problems it still had, and whether it seemed worth refining again under the assignment constraints.
* We used those discussions to shape the next prompt each time and kept moving until the experiment rounds were complete.

**4. Writing Prompts and Results Files**
* As we worked through the rounds, we also wrote the refinement prompts and the results markdown files along the way.
* We did this during the meeting instead of leaving it all for later so we would not forget why certain candidates were selected.
* When writing the prompts, we tried to keep them focused and within the assignment rules while still making them useful for the next round.
* The results files were treated as part of the actual experiment record, since they captured the reasoning behind our choices.

**5. Wrapping Up and Dividing Remaining Work**
* By the end of the meeting, we had finished the experimental portion of the assignment, including the later refinement stages.
* Once that was done, we talked about what still needed to happen before submission.
* The main things left were putting together the presentation and making sure everything was properly uploaded and organized in the GitHub repository.
* We decided those remaining tasks would be handled by the other group members after the meeting.

## Decisions Made
* **Reviewed the initial 50 runs as a group:** We used the in-person meeting to compare the baseline results together before making any refinement decisions.
  * **Reasoning:** It was easier to evaluate the differences between candidates once everyone could look at them side by side and talk through them.

* **Finished all remaining refinement rounds during the meeting:** Instead of stopping after one stage, we worked through the rest of the iteration process in this session.
  * **Reasoning:** We already had momentum, and it made sense to finish the experiment while the group was together and actively comparing results.

* **Wrote prompts and results files during the process:** We created the refinement prompts and results markdown files as each round happened.
  * **Reasoning:** This helped us keep an accurate record of what we were thinking at each stage instead of trying to reconstruct it later.

* **Delegated final packaging tasks to the rest of the team:** After the experimentation work was done, we left the presentation and repo cleanup/upload work to the remaining members.
  * **Reasoning:** The group present at the meeting had already completed the main experiment work, so splitting up the last tasks was the easiest way to finish everything.
